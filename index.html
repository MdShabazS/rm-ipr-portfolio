01 · Problem Statement

The Hidden Threat Beneath Our Feet

General Description

Landmines buried in post-conflict zones continue to threaten civilian lives and render vast tracts of land unusable for agriculture, infrastructure, and resettlement — often decades after conflicts end. With an estimated 110 million active landmines spread across 60+ countries, the scale of the problem demands a smarter, safer response.

Current methods depend on trained deminers physically traversing suspect areas with handheld detectors — a process that is slow, expensive, and profoundly dangerous. Existing semi-automated systems still require operators near hazardous zones, offering only marginal safety improvement.

Importance & Context

The humanitarian and economic consequences are severe: lost lives, amputations, widespread trauma, and land that cannot be farmed or inhabited. There is a critical need for a solution that is technically sound and practically deployable in resource-constrained environments. An autonomous aerial system directly addresses the core problem: the need for humans to be physically present in danger zones.

Team Reflection

What sparked our interest was the persistent reality that landmine casualties are not historical — they are ongoing. We initially framed the problem narrowly as a hardware challenge. Inputs from our Guide broadened this: the real bottleneck is not sensor capability alone, but safe autonomous deployment and reliable data fusion. The PRC review pushed us further, raising practical deployment constraints — terrain variation, battery endurance, and the cost of false negatives — which significantly deepened how we defined the problem.

02 · Objectives

What We Set Out to Achieve

01
Design and develop an autonomous drone system that scans suspected landmine fields by following a pre-programmed flight path without any manual intervention during operation.
02
Detect subsurface landmines accurately by integrating a metal detector coil and thermal camera with a sensor fusion algorithm that minimises false detections.
03
Geo-tag and map every detected threat using a GPS module and display real-time detection data on a ground control station dashboard accessible from a safe distance.
04
Eliminate human exposure during landmine survey operations by replacing dangerous manual ground sweeping with a fully autonomous aerial detection system.
Team Reflection

Our initial objectives were broad and aspirational — effectively "build a drone that finds mines." The PRC sessions pushed us to make objectives measurable and bounded: specifying what "accurate" means (sensor fusion with defined false-positive thresholds) and what "safe distance" entails operationally. Our Guide helped us recognise that the geo-tagging and real-time dashboard objective was critical: detection without actionable, mapped data has limited field utility.

Team Reflection

Reviewing existing literature revealed the core trade-off landscape: single-sensor approaches are cheaper and simpler but unreliable; multi-sensor fusion improves accuracy but introduces complexity and power constraints. The 2023 CNN paper was particularly notable — it demonstrated AI classification potential while exposing data scarcity as a real barrier in niche domains. The key pattern across all six papers: no single work had achieved full autonomy with real-time geo-mapping at low cost. That absence pointed us toward our own direction.

04 · Research Gap

Where Current Work Falls Short

Gap 01

No existing low-cost system combines metal detection and thermal sensing with full autonomous aerial navigation and real-time geo-mapping in a single integrated platform.

Gap 02

Current multi-sensor systems lack fusion logic specifically tuned to reduce false positives in varied terrain conditions without requiring high computational resources.

Gap 03

Most UAV-based detection research is conducted in controlled environments; practical deployment constraints such as battery endurance during area scanning missions are under-studied.

Gap 04

Existing systems do not provide an operator-accessible ground control dashboard aggregating GPS-tagged detection events in real time, limiting their field operational utility.

Team Reflection

Distinguishing between a "gap" and a "limitation" required careful thought. A limitation is a known constraint of an existing approach — thermal imaging struggling with deep mines. A gap is an unaddressed opportunity that no current work has adequately resolved. Limitations are the ceiling of existing methods; gaps are the floor of what remains to be built. Our Guide helped us understand that identifying a gap only holds value if our proposed work meaningfully addresses it — which forced us to connect each gap directly to a specific design decision in MineGuard.

05 · Proposed Solution

MineGuard: A Conceptual Framework

High-Level Concept

MineGuard is an autonomous aerial detection platform. A drone equipped with a dual-sensor payload follows a pre-programmed grid flight path over a suspect area. Onboard, signals from both sensors are cross-validated. When a threat signature is confirmed, the system records the GPS coordinates and transmits a real-time alert to a ground control station — with no human required to enter the area.

The system operates across three functional layers: Navigate (autonomous GPS-guided area coverage), Detect (multi-sensor data acquisition and fusion), and Report (geo-tagging, threat mapping, and GCS transmission).

Team Reflection

The dual-sensor fusion concept emerged directly from the literature: no single sensor is sufficient. Patel et al. (2019) proved that combining sensors improves results despite added complexity — we built on that rather than reinventing from scratch. Alternatives considered and rejected: GPR (too heavy and expensive for a small frame), chemical sniffers (too immature for reliable deployment), and visual-only cameras (ineffective for buried objects). The metal-thermal combination offered the best balance of cost, weight, and detection coverage for our scope and budget.

06 · Methodology

Our Conceptual Approach

1
Problem Definition & Requirements Analysis
Define operational requirements, safety constraints, area coverage specifications, and detection accuracy targets before any component selection.
2
Component Selection & System Architecture
Select the drone frame, ESP32 microcontroller, metal detector coil, thermal camera, GPS module, and RF communication module based on weight and power budgets.
3
Autonomous Navigation Design
Design the waypoint-based grid flight path algorithm and integrate GPS-guided navigation to ensure complete coverage of the target area.
4
Sensor Integration & Fusion Logic
Mount sensors on the drone frame in parallel with developing the fusion algorithm that cross-validates metal and thermal signals to reduce false positives.
5
Ground Control Station Development
Build the GCS dashboard to receive real-time RF data, display GPS-tagged threat locations on a map, and log all detection events.
6
Testing, Validation & Iteration
Conduct controlled trials, analyse detection accuracy, identify failure modes, and iterate on fusion parameters and navigation coverage patterns.
Team Reflection

Structuring the methodology was harder than anticipated. Our first draft was a vague sequence of "plan, build, test." Our Guide introduced a useful reframe: think in terms of dependencies — what must exist before the next step can begin? That question transformed a loose list into a structured pipeline. The biggest challenge was placing sensor fusion development correctly: our mentor clarified it should run in parallel with hardware integration, not after it, enabling iterative calibration. This was an insight we would not have reached independently.
